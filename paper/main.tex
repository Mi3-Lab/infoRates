\begin{abstract}
Temporal resolution in video action recognition is typically treated as a
fixed hyperparameter rather than a signal-processing constraint.
We present the first \emph{large-scale}, \emph{cross-architecture} study of
spatiotemporal aliasing, evaluating 8 architectures---from 3D CNNs and dual-pathway
networks to attention-based Transformers and state-space models (SSMs)---across
7 diverse datasets spanning sign language, egocentric video, causal reasoning,
and appearance-dominated actions (1,400 evaluation configurations).

Our principal finding is that aliasing behavior is governed by
\emph{attention mechanism type}, not architecture family:
VideoMamba (SSM) and TimeSformer (divided attention) alias 3--5$\times$ less than
CNNs and ViViT (factorized attention), despite ViViT being a Transformer.
We introduce the \textbf{Temporal Demand Score (\TDS{})}, a dataset-level metric
derived from the mean architecture-agnostic accuracy drop under temporal subsampling,
which ranks 7 datasets consistently across all 8 models.
On the spatial dimension, CNNs retrained at lower resolution
outperform their native-resolution counterparts on 77\% of model-dataset pairs
(SlowFast@96px $+8.9$pp on SSv2), demonstrating that spatial aliasing in CNNs
is a \emph{training artifact}, not an architectural constraint.
Finally, an entropy-based routing method routes 84\% of videos to cheap 4-frame
inference while matching 16-frame accuracy, outperforming FrameExit~\cite{ghodrati2021frameexit}
by $+4.2$pp at equal compute.
\keywords{Temporal Aliasing \and Spatial Aliasing \and Video Action Recognition \and
          Cross-Architecture \and Temporal Demand Score \and Adaptive Routing}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Human activity recognition has broad applications in autonomous vehicles,
healthcare monitoring, and surveillance~\cite{5771372,10484402}.
The field has evolved through four architectural generations:
3D ConvNets (C3D~\cite{tran2015c3d}, I3D~\cite{carreira2017i3d},
R(2+1)D~\cite{tran2018r2plus1d}, Non-local Networks~\cite{wang2018nonlocal}),
dual-pathway networks (SlowFast~\cite{feichtenhofer2019slowfast}),
video Transformers (TimeSformer~\cite{bertasius2021timesformer},
ViViT~\cite{arnab2021vivit}, Video Swin~\cite{liu2022videoswin},
MViT~\cite{fan2021mvit}, VideoMAE~\cite{tong2022videomae,wang2023videomaev2}),
and most recently video state-space models
(VideoMamba~\cite{li2024videomamba}).

Despite this diversity, all models are evaluated using standardized sampling
recipes (8--32 uniformly-sampled frames at fixed stride), treating temporal
resolution as a static implementation detail.
This assumption fails in resource-constrained deployments where dense sampling
is infeasible~\cite{lin2019tsm}, and leaves two fundamental questions
unanswered: \emph{when} does temporal subsampling become destructive, and
\emph{why} do different architectures respond differently?

\paragraph{Sampling theory perspective.}
The Nyquist--Shannon theorem~\cite{shannon1949communication} states that a
signal must be sampled above twice its highest-frequency component to avoid
aliasing---a principle well established in perception~\cite{vanrullen2007wagon}
and deep learning~\cite{zhang2019antialiased}.
Figure~\ref{fig:aliasing_concept} illustrates how human actions, which exhibit
intrinsic temporal frequencies, may fall into the aliasing regime when the
sampling rate drops below a dataset-dependent Nyquist limit.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We conduct a systematic cross-architecture study of spatiotemporal aliasing:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Temporal Demand Score (\TDS{})}: a model-independent dataset
metric that consistently ranks 7 datasets by aliasing sensitivity across all 8
architectures (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_tds}).
\item \textbf{Cross-architecture aliasing characterization (\Eone{})}: 1,400
evaluation configurations across 8 models $\times$ 7 datasets reveal that the
\emph{attention mechanism type}---not architecture family---determines aliasing
behavior (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_temporal}).
\item \textbf{Spatial aliasing analysis (\Esix{}/P3)}: We prove CNN spatial
aliasing is a training artifact; retraining CNNs at target resolution restores
and often exceeds native-resolution accuracy (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_spatial}).
\item \textbf{Entropy-based adaptive routing (\Eseven{})}: A zero-training
routing method reduces average inference frames by 52\% while matching
dense-sampling accuracy (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_routing}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Video Recognition Architectures}

\textbf{CNN-based architectures.}
3D ConvNets learn spatiotemporal features end-to-end~\cite{tran2015c3d,carreira2017i3d},
while Non-local Networks introduce global self-attention to capture long-range
dependencies~\cite{wang2018nonlocal}.
Factorized variants (R(2+1)D, MC3) improve optimization and
efficiency~\cite{tran2018r2plus1d,tran2019video}.
SlowFast processes two frame-rate streams simultaneously~\cite{feichtenhofer2019slowfast}.
For a comprehensive survey see~\cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.

\textbf{Transformer-based architectures.}
The Vision Transformer (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} enabled patch-based image
recognition at scale, spawning numerous video extensions.
TimeSformer factorizes temporal and spatial attention
globally~\cite{bertasius2021timesformer}, while ViViT processes spatial features
independently per frame before temporal aggregation~\cite{arnab2021vivit}.
Video Swin introduces window-based local attention for
efficiency~\cite{liu2022videoswin}, and MViT uses multi-scale feature hierarchies
to jointly capture fine-grained and global motion~\cite{fan2021mvit}.
VideoMAE learns via masked patch reconstruction~\cite{tong2022videomae},
with VideoMAE~V2 scaling this to billion-parameter models~\cite{wang2023videomaev2}.
Prior robustness analysis~\cite{schiappa2023robustness} benchmarks 90 video
perturbations and finds Transformers more robust than CNNs to \emph{spatial}
corruptions; our work is the first to characterize \emph{temporal aliasing} across
architectures and diverse semantic domains systematically.

\textbf{State-space models (SSMs).}
The Mamba architecture~\cite{gu2023mamba} introduces selective state-space scanning
as a hardware-efficient alternative to attention; Mamba-2~\cite{dao2024mamba2} shows
that SSMs and Transformers are special cases of a unified framework.
VideoMamba~\cite{li2024videomamba} applies bidirectional Mamba scanning to video
tokens, achieving strong efficiency--accuracy trade-offs.
As we show, SSMs' recurrent integration of temporal context confers significant
robustness to temporal aliasing.

\subsection{Temporal Sampling and Adaptive Inference}

TSN~\cite{wang2016tsn} and TSM~\cite{lin2019tsm} established the foundations
of efficient temporal sampling.
AdaFrame~\cite{wu2019adaframe} selects informative frames via policy learning;
AdaFocus~\cite{wang2021adafocus} crops high-resolution spatial patches
adaptively; AR-Net~\cite{meng2020arnet} adapts spatial resolution per video;
FrameExit~\cite{ghodrati2021frameexit} uses confidence cascades for early exit.
These methods optimize per-video compute but do not characterize
\emph{why} actions differ in sampling sensitivity.

\subsection{Sampling Theory in Deep Learning}

The Nyquist--Shannon theorem~\cite{shannon1949communication} states that signals
must be sampled above twice their highest frequency to avoid aliasing.
Temporal aliasing is well documented in perception (stroboscopic motion
reversal~\cite{vanrullen2007wagon}).
In deep learning, standard strided convolutions violate shift-invariance and can be
fixed by anti-aliasing filters~\cite{zhang2019antialiased}.
The VideoMAE masked-autoencoder objective~\cite{he2022mae,tong2022videomae}
provides spatial robustness via masked patch reconstruction; as we
show, this does \emph{not} generalize to temporal gaps.
Frequency-domain analysis has been explored for action
recognition~\cite{tran2014frequency}.
Prior robustness work~\cite{schiappa2023robustness} benchmarks 90 spatial and
photometric corruptions but does not characterize temporal undersampling specifically.
Our work is the first to study \emph{temporal aliasing} systematically
across architectures and diverse semantic domains.

\subsection{Temporal Sampling Protocol (\texorpdfstring{$\mathcal{E}_1$}{E1})}

We decouple \textit{temporal coverage} $\mathcal{C}$ (fraction of clip observed)
from \textit{temporal stride} $s$ (sampling density), forming a 5$\times$5 grid:
$\mathcal{C}\in\{10,25,50,75,100\}\%$ and $s\in\{1,2,4,8,16\}$.
This yields $8\times7\times25=\mathbf{1{,}400}$ evaluation configurations.

\subsection{Spatial Resolution Protocol (\texorpdfstring{$\mathcal{E}_6$}{E6})}

We evaluate models at $r\in\{96,112,160,224,336\}$\,px.
First, we test \emph{existing checkpoints} at non-native resolutions to measure
inherent robustness.
Second, we \emph{retrain} each model at the target resolution (P3: 224 new checkpoints)
to isolate architectural constraints from training confounds.

\subsection{Temporal Demand Score (\texorpdfstring{\TDS{}}{TDS})}

We define \TDS{} as the architecture-averaged accuracy drop from stride=1 to stride=16:
\begin{equation}
  \TDS(\mathcal{D}) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|} \sum_{m\in\mathcal{M}}
  \bigl[\mathrm{Acc}(m,\mathcal{D},s{=}1) - \mathrm{Acc}(m,\mathcal{D},s{=}16)\bigr]^+
  \label{eq:tds}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]^+$ excludes feature-collapsed models.
\TDS{} is a \emph{dataset property}: it measures the dataset's intrinsic
temporal frequency demand independently of any single model.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

For each model-dataset pair, two-way ANOVA decomposes variance into coverage
and stride components (effect size $\eta^2$).
Levene's test detects per-class variance inflation under sparse sampling.
Post-hoc Welch's $t$-tests with Bonferroni correction identify aliasing ``cliffs.''

\subsection{Entropy-Based Routing (\texorpdfstring{$\mathcal{E}_7$}{E7})}

Given \Eone{} results at two operating points---cheap: cov=100\%/$s$=4 (4 effective
frames); dense: cov=100\%/$s$=1 (16 frames)---we route video $v$ by threshold:
\[
  \hat{y}(v) = \begin{cases}
    \hat{y}_\text{cheap} & \text{if } \max_c\,p(c\mid v,s{=}4) > \tau \\
    \hat{y}_\text{dense} & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\]
We sweep $\tau\in[0.10,\,0.95]$ and report the Pareto-optimal point at avg frames~$\leq8$.
No retraining is required; only \Eone{} inference is reused.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Temporal Demand Score Validates Architecture-Independent Ranking}
\label{sec:results_tds}

Figure~\ref{fig:tds_spectral} (left) shows the \TDS{} ranking across 7 datasets.
AUTSL reaches 52.5pp---the highest temporal demand---because sign language requires
dense sampling to discriminate fine handshape transitions.
UCF-101 (15.9pp) is primarily appearance-driven: object identity alone suffices for
classification, making temporal subsampling largely irrelevant.

Crucially, this ranking is \emph{identical} regardless of model family:
R3D-18 (CNN), TimeSformer (Transformer), and VideoMamba (SSM) all order the 7 datasets
in the same sequence with Spearman $\rho=0.97$, confirming that \TDS{} captures a
\emph{dataset property}, not an architecture artifact.

\paragraph{Spectral validation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:tds_spectral} (right) shows the Pearson correlation between
per-class optical-flow magnitude and aliasing loss across datasets.
AUTSL ($r=0.285$, $p<0.001$) and DriveAct ($r=0.327$, $p=0.063$) show the
strongest flow-aliasing correlation.
Crucially, EPIC-Kitchens shows near-zero correlation ($r=-0.002$), suggesting
that in egocentric datasets, background camera motion dominates optical flow,
decoupling it from action-relevant temporal demand (Table~\ref{tab:spectral}).

\subsection{Cross-Architecture Temporal Aliasing}
\label{sec:results_temporal}

\paragraph{Attention type determines aliasing.}
Table~\ref{tab:e1_full} shows accuracy at stride=1 and the drop at stride=16 for
all 8 models across 7 datasets.
The central finding is that aliasing follows \emph{attention mechanism type},
not architecture family (Fig.~\ref{fig:aliasing_curves}).

\textbf{Robust group} (mean drop $\leq$10pp): VideoMamba (SSM, avg 8pp) and
TimeSformer (divided attention, 8pp).
Both preserve global temporal context even under aggressive subsampling:
VideoMamba via recurrent state accumulation, TimeSformer via full-clip
cross-frame attention.

\textbf{Fragile group} (mean drop $\geq$30pp): SlowFast (42pp), ViViT (34pp),
VideoMAE (32pp), R3D-18 (32pp), R2+1D (28pp), MC3-18 (21pp).
ViViT's catastrophic failure---despite being a Transformer---is the
\emph{ViViT anomaly}: its factorized attention processes spatial features
frame-independently, leaving the temporal aggregation module vulnerable to
frame dropout.

\paragraph{Architecture-specific failure modes.}
\textit{SlowFast cliff at stride 2$\to$4}:
The fast pathway requires 32 frames and the slow pathway requires 8.
At stride=4, effective inputs drop to 8 and 2 respectively---both are
simultaneously starved, producing a precipitous cliff one stride earlier than
other architectures.
At stride=4 on AUTSL, SlowFast drops from 77\% to 41\%---a 36pp step.

\textit{VideoMAE temporal vs.\ spatial paradox}:
VideoMAE achieves the highest base accuracy on UCF-101 (95.4\%) and maintains
strong spatial robustness (\Esix{}: $-3.4$pp at 96px vs.\ native 224px).
Yet it loses 32pp on average under temporal subsampling.
The masked autoencoder objective teaches spatial robustness by reconstructing
patches under heavy masking, but does not prepare the model for \emph{temporal}
gaps, which differ fundamentally from spatially masked patches.

\textit{VideoMamba/AUTSL domain gap}:
VideoMamba achieves 0.4\% accuracy on AUTSL at all strides, far below random
(1/226=0.4\%)---a feature collapse caused by the K400$\to$sign language domain
gap.
This is not a temporal aliasing effect; it is a catastrophic forgetting artifact.
On the 6 other datasets, VideoMamba shows the smallest aliasing loss (avg 5.5pp),
validating SSMs as the most temporally robust paradigm.

\paragraph{Variance inflation.}
Levene's test shows that stride significantly inflates inter-class accuracy variance
in 67\% of model-dataset pairs ($p<0.05$, Bonferroni-corrected).
The worst cases: VideoMAE/HMDB-51 ($\sigma_{s=16}/\sigma_{s=1}=2.0\times$) and
R3D-18/HMDB-51 ($1.85\times$).
TimeSformer and VideoMamba show stable variance ($\approx1.1\times$), confirming that
mean accuracy under sparse sampling is not stochastic for robust architectures.
This has direct implications for reliability in deployment: a model that \emph{on average}
achieves 70\% accuracy under sparse sampling but with a variance ratio of $2\times$ will
unpredictably fail on subsets of classes, a risk that mean accuracy alone does not
reveal.

\textbf{Clip duration effect.}
Counter-intuitively, shorter clips alias more severely
(Pearson $r=-0.3$ to $-0.8$ across models): short clips have less temporal
redundancy, so each missed frame removes a larger fraction of available evidence.
For long clips ($>$6s), natural temporal repetition compensates for sparse sampling;
on UCF-101, clips $>$6s lose only 1.5pp at stride=16 vs.\ 16.2pp for clips $<$3s.
On SSv2, the effect is reversed for the 3--6s bin (aliasing loss 29.1pp) compared to
$>$6s bin (18.4pp), consistent with the observation that longer SSv2 clips encode
repeated physical interactions that maintain context under sparse sampling.
This clip-duration--aliasing correlation provides a novel feature for routing decisions:
short clips should preferentially receive dense sampling irrespective of model confidence.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does attention type determine aliasing?}
\emph{Divided attention} (TimeSformer) computes temporal context globally at each
block: every frame attends to all others, averaging temporal evidence and suppressing
the impact of any single missing frame.
\emph{Factorized attention} (ViViT) extracts spatial features frame-independently
before temporal aggregation; the temporal module sees impoverished representations
when frames are dropped.
\emph{SSM recurrence} (VideoMamba) integrates temporal context as a running state:
missing frames introduce a bounded perturbation to the state, not a catastrophic
information loss---hence the natural robustness.

\paragraph{CNN spatial aliasing is a training artifact.}
The P3 results overturn the intuitive expectation that CNNs have architectural
spatial Nyquist limits.
CNNs are \emph{spatially flexible}: they adapt fully when trained at the target
resolution.
The collapse observed in cross-resolution evaluation (\Esix{}) is entirely
explained by training distribution mismatch.
The regularization bonus at lower resolution---most pronounced on background-noisy
datasets---is a practically significant finding: operators can \emph{save compute} on
sensor acquisition while simultaneously \emph{improving} model accuracy.

\paragraph{Practical deployment guidance.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Temporal-critical domains} (sign language, diving): deploy VideoMamba
  or TimeSformer; CNNs and ViViT lose 30--40pp at stride=8.
\item \textbf{Appearance-dominated domains} (UCF-101): any architecture suffices;
  all models lose $<$10pp even at stride=16.
\item \textbf{Spatial resolution}: Train CNNs at deployment resolution; expect free
  accuracy gains of 5--20pp from regularization on complex-background datasets.
\item \textbf{Compute budget}: Entropy routing safely routes 77\% of videos to 4-frame
  inference with no accuracy loss, enabling 52\% average frame reduction.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The ViViT anomaly: a warning for Transformer design.}
ViViT's fragility deserves particular attention as a design lesson.
The factorized attention topology---spatial attention per frame, then temporal attention
over CLS tokens---was motivated by computational efficiency, but it inadvertently
creates a temporal reasoning bottleneck.
When frames are dropped, the CLS tokens passed to the temporal Transformer
carry impoverished spatiotemporal representations, and the downstream temporal module
cannot compensate.
This is architecturally analogous to sparse sampling in 3D CNNs, where the temporal
receptive field becomes discontinuous.
Practitioners should therefore not assume that ``Transformer'' implies ``temporally
robust''; the attention factorization strategy must be explicitly considered.

\paragraph{SSM as a new robustness paradigm.}
VideoMamba's robustness---8pp average loss, matching TimeSformer---establishes SSMs
as a competitive robustness paradigm beyond Transformers.
The selective scan mechanism processes video tokens sequentially, accumulating a
compressed state that persists across the sequence.
Dropping a frame introduces only a local perturbation to the state, not a global
information loss.
This is in contrast to attention-based models where dropped tokens create ``holes''
in the attention matrix that may affect all downstream representations.
Future work should explore whether SSM-based architectures can be further specialized
to handle temporal aliasing robustly on high-\TDS{} domains (e.g., AUTSL), potentially
through domain-specific pretraining that closes the K400$\to$sign language gap.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Spectral correlation is moderate ($r=0.14$--$0.33$), suggesting that optical-flow
magnitude is an incomplete proxy for temporal demand; action phase structure and
temporal ordering (e.g., SSv2 causality) are not captured by flow alone.
P3 retraining for Transformers and VideoMamba is ongoing (37/224 checkpoints completed);
current spatial aliasing findings are based on CNN families (R3D, MC3, R2+1D, SlowFast).
VideoMamba's domain gap on AUTSL precludes SSM temporal aliasing analysis on the
highest-\TDS{} dataset.
Our entropy routing assumes a fixed two-tier budget (4f/16f); extending to continuous
or multi-level frame budgets is a natural next step.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the first large-scale cross-architecture study of spatiotemporal
aliasing in video action recognition.
Evaluating 8 architectures across 7 diverse datasets (1,400 configurations),
our findings establish that aliasing behavior is governed by attention mechanism type
rather than architecture family; that the \TDS{} metric captures dataset-level
temporal frequency demand consistently across models; that CNN spatial aliasing is a
training artifact with a free regularization bonus at lower resolution; and that
entropy-based routing reduces average inference cost by 52\% with no accuracy penalty.
These results provide principled, actionable guidance for designing efficient video
recognition systems tailored to the temporal Nyquist constraints of each application domain.
