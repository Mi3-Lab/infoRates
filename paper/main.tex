\begin{abstract}
Temporal resolution in video action recognition is typically treated as a fixed hyperparameter rather than a signal-processing constraint. We present a \emph{large-scale}, \emph{cross-architecture} study of spatiotemporal aliasing, evaluating 8 architectures---from 3D CNNs and dual-pathway networks to attention-based Transformers and state-space models (SSMs)---across 8 diverse datasets spanning sign language, fine-grained gymnastics, egocentric video, causal reasoning, and appearance-dominated actions (8,000+ evaluation configurations: 5 resolutions $\times$ 5 coverage levels $\times$ 5 temporal strides per architecture--dataset pair).

Our principal finding is that aliasing behavior is strongly associated with \emph{attention mechanism type} rather than architecture family: VideoMamba (SSM) and TimeSformer (divided attention) alias 3--5$\times$ less than CNNs and ViViT (factorized attention), despite ViViT being a Transformer. We introduce the \textbf{Temporal Demand Score (\TDS{})}, a dataset-level metric derived from the mean architecture-agnostic accuracy drop under temporal subsampling, which ranks 8 datasets consistently across all 8 models (Spearman $\rho{=}0.97$). FineGym (fine-grained gymnastics, \TDS{}=55.9pp) rivals sign language (AUTSL, 53.0pp) as the highest-demand domain---the two are close enough that their relative order is sensitive to which architectures are included in the average---establishing sport phase transitions as a previously underappreciated high-aliasing regime. Cross-resolution evaluation shows CNNs degrade up to 73pp off-native while Transformers stay within $\pm$8pp on 7 of 8 datasets (VideoMamba is more variable, from $\pm$6pp to $-23$pp); target-resolution fine-tuning recovers up to 39pp for CNNs while leaving Transformers largely unchanged. Measured GPU latency reveals that VideoMAE is the most efficient Transformer (1.6--2.8ms across resolutions) while VideoMamba is 4--8$\times$ slower than comparable models (8--15ms), directly informing Nyquist-aware deployment choices. Finally, entropy-based routing reduces average inference cost by 52\% with no accuracy penalty, outperforming FrameExit~\cite{ghodrati2021frameexit} by $+4.2$pp. All 8,000+ evaluation results and an interactive dashboard are available at \url{https://mi3-inforates.streamlit.app/} (code repository: anonymized for review).
\keywords{Temporal Aliasing \and Spatial Aliasing \and Video Action Recognition \and
          Cross-Architecture \and Temporal Demand Score \and Adaptive Routing}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Human activity recognition has broad applications in autonomous vehicles, healthcare monitoring, and surveillance~\cite{5771372,10484402}. The field has evolved through four architectural generations: 3D ConvNets (C3D~\cite{tran2015c3d}, I3D~\cite{carreira2017i3d}, R(2+1)D~\cite{tran2018r2plus1d}, Non-local Networks~\cite{wang2018nonlocal}), dual-pathway networks (SlowFast~\cite{feichtenhofer2019slowfast}), video Transformers (TimeSformer~\cite{bertasius2021timesformer}, ViViT~\cite{arnab2021vivit}, Video Swin~\cite{liu2022videoswin}, MViT~\cite{fan2021mvit}, VideoMAE~\cite{tong2022videomae,wang2023videomaev2}), and most recently video state-space models (VideoMamba~\cite{li2024videomamba}).

Despite this diversity, all models are evaluated using standardized sampling recipes (8--32 uniformly-sampled frames at fixed stride), treating temporal resolution as a static implementation detail. This assumption fails in resource-constrained deployments where dense sampling is infeasible~\cite{lin2019tsm}, and leaves two fundamental questions unanswered: \emph{when} does temporal subsampling become destructive, and \emph{why} do different architectures respond differently?

\paragraph{Sampling theory perspective.}
The Nyquist--Shannon theorem~\cite{shannon1949communication} states that a signal must be sampled above twice its highest-frequency component to avoid aliasing---a principle well established in perception~\cite{vanrullen2007wagon} and deep learning~\cite{zhang2019antialiased}. Figure~\ref{fig:aliasing_concept} illustrates how human actions, which exhibit intrinsic temporal frequencies, may fall into the aliasing regime when the sampling rate drops below a dataset-dependent Nyquist limit.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We conduct a systematic cross-architecture study of spatiotemporal aliasing:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Temporal Demand Score (\TDS{})}: an architecture-averaged dataset
metric that consistently ranks 8 datasets by aliasing sensitivity, validated by leave-one-architecture-out analysis (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_tds}). FineGym (55.9pp) rivals sign language (AUTSL, 53.0pp) as the highest-demand domain.
\item \textbf{Cross-architecture aliasing characterization}: 8,000+ evaluation
configurations reveal that \emph{attention mechanism type}---not architecture family---is the strongest correlate of aliasing behavior, with VideoMamba and TimeSformer aliasing 3--5$\times$ less than CNNs and ViViT (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_temporal}).
\item \textbf{Target-resolution fine-tuning}: Fine-tuning each model at the deployment
resolution closes the cross-resolution gap by up to 39pp for CNNs while Transformers---already resolution-robust---require no retraining (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_spatial}).
\item \textbf{Measured inference latency}: GPU latency characterization
across all 8 models $\times$ 5 resolutions (bfloat16), enabling Nyquist-aware deployment budgeting per device class (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_latency}).
\item \textbf{Entropy-based adaptive routing}: A zero-training method reduces
average inference frames by 52\% while matching dense-sampling accuracy, outperforming FrameExit by $+4.2$pp (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_routing}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Video Recognition Architectures}

\textbf{CNN-based architectures.} 3D ConvNets learn spatiotemporal features end-to-end~\cite{tran2015c3d,carreira2017i3d}, while Non-local Networks introduce global self-attention to capture long-range dependencies~\cite{wang2018nonlocal}. Factorized variants (R(2+1)D, MC3) improve optimization and efficiency~\cite{tran2018r2plus1d,tran2019video}. SlowFast processes two frame-rate streams simultaneously~\cite{feichtenhofer2019slowfast}. For a comprehensive survey see~\cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.

\textbf{Transformer-based architectures.} The Vision Transformer (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} enabled patch-based image recognition at scale, spawning numerous video extensions. TimeSformer factorizes temporal and spatial attention globally~\cite{bertasius2021timesformer}, while ViViT processes spatial features independently per frame before temporal aggregation~\cite{arnab2021vivit}. Video Swin introduces window-based local attention for efficiency~\cite{liu2022videoswin}, and MViT uses multi-scale feature hierarchies to jointly capture fine-grained and global motion~\cite{fan2021mvit}. VideoMAE learns via masked patch reconstruction~\cite{tong2022videomae}, with VideoMAE~V2 scaling this to billion-parameter models~\cite{wang2023videomaev2}. Prior robustness analysis~\cite{schiappa2023robustness} benchmarks 90 video perturbations and finds Transformers more robust than CNNs to \emph{spatial} corruptions; we extend this line of work to systematically characterize \emph{temporal aliasing} across architectures and diverse semantic domains.

\textbf{State-space models (SSMs).} The Mamba architecture~\cite{gu2023mamba} introduces selective state-space scanning as a hardware-efficient alternative to attention; Mamba-2~\cite{dao2024mamba2} shows that SSMs and Transformers are special cases of a unified framework. VideoMamba~\cite{li2024videomamba} applies bidirectional Mamba scanning to video tokens, achieving strong efficiency--accuracy trade-offs. As we show, SSMs' recurrent integration of temporal context confers significant robustness to temporal aliasing, though GPU latency is higher than expected.

\subsection{Temporal Sampling and Adaptive Inference}

TSN~\cite{wang2016tsn} and TSM~\cite{lin2019tsm} established the foundations of efficient temporal sampling. AdaFrame~\cite{wu2019adaframe} selects informative frames via policy learning; AdaFocus~\cite{wang2021adafocus} crops high-resolution spatial patches adaptively; AR-Net~\cite{meng2020arnet} adapts spatial resolution per video; FrameExit~\cite{ghodrati2021frameexit} uses confidence cascades for early exit. Huang \emph{et al.}~\cite{huang2018makes} study which datasets require temporal reasoning by testing models on shuffled frames, finding that many ``temporal'' datasets are solved by appearance cues alone; Sevilla-Lara \emph{et al.}~\cite{sevilla2021only} show that motion-only cues improve performance primarily on action sports---a finding our \TDS{} ranking corroborates for sign language and gymnastics. These analyses are qualitative; we provide a quantitative, architecture-averaged characterization of temporal sensitivity via the \TDS{} metric (Sec.~\ref{sec:results_tds} discusses its dependence on the evaluated model pool).

\subsection{Sampling Theory in Deep Learning}

The Nyquist--Shannon theorem~\cite{shannon1949communication} states that signals must be sampled above twice their highest frequency to avoid aliasing. Temporal aliasing is well documented in perception (stroboscopic motion reversal~\cite{vanrullen2007wagon}). In deep learning, standard strided convolutions violate shift-invariance and can be fixed by anti-aliasing filters~\cite{zhang2019antialiased}. The VideoMAE masked-autoencoder objective~\cite{he2022mae,tong2022videomae} provides spatial robustness via masked patch reconstruction; as we show, this does \emph{not} generalize to temporal gaps. Frequency-domain analysis has been explored for action recognition~\cite{tran2014frequency}. Prior robustness work~\cite{schiappa2023robustness} benchmarks 90 spatial and photometric corruptions but does not characterize temporal undersampling specifically. Our work is the first to study \emph{temporal aliasing} systematically across architectures and diverse semantic domains.

\subsection{Temporal Sampling Protocol}

We decouple \textit{temporal coverage} $\mathcal{C}$ (fraction of clip observed) from \textit{temporal stride} $s$ (sampling density), forming a 5$\times$5 grid: $\mathcal{C}\in\{10,25,50,75,100\}\%$ and $s\in\{1,2,4,8,16\}$. The temporal sweep at native resolution yields $8{\times}8{\times}25{=}\mathbf{1{,}600}$ model--dataset--configuration triples (each evaluated over ${\approx}20$ clips per class). Combined with the spatial resolution sweep (5 resolutions), the total reaches \textbf{8,000+ evaluation configurations}.

\paragraph{Coverage implementation.}
Coverage is applied from the \emph{beginning} of each clip: the first $\lfloor T\cdot\mathcal{C}/100\rfloor$ frames form the candidate pool, and $s$ controls sampling density within that window. This simulates a sensor that observes only a leading fraction of the action and gives the sharpest, most interpretable measurement of ``how early in the clip does the model have enough temporal context.'' An alternative---random window placement---would confound temporal structure with missing-data effects, making coverage and stride effects harder to disentangle. We acknowledge that this choice is conservative for datasets where key events occur at clip boundaries (e.g., action completion in SSv2); supplementary~S1 shows that the coverage effect on SSv2 is qualitatively similar across window positions when checked on a 10\% random subset.

\subsection{Spatial Resolution Protocol}

\paragraph{Cross-resolution evaluation.}
We evaluate all 8 fine-tuned checkpoints (at native resolution) on \emph{all 8 datasets} at $r\in\{48,96,112,160,224,336\}$\,px \emph{without retraining}, measuring accuracy retention when the input resolution differs from the native training resolution (112px for CNNs, 224px for Transformers and VideoMamba).

\paragraph{Target-resolution fine-tuning.}
For each (model, dataset, resolution) triple at $r\in\{48,96,112,160,224\}$\,px, we fine-tune from the native-resolution checkpoint for 10 additional epochs. For Transformers and VideoMamba, positional embeddings are bicubically interpolated from the native grid to the target grid before fine-tuning; passing \texttt{ignore\_mismatched\_sizes=True} in HuggingFace discards positional embeddings entirely and causes a large accuracy cliff---our interpolation-based approach avoids this. CNNs transfer directly; SlowFast head pooling is replaced with \texttt{AdaptiveAvgPool3d(1)} to support non-native resolutions.

\subsection{Temporal Demand Score (\texorpdfstring{\TDS{}}{TDS})}

We define \TDS{} as the architecture-averaged accuracy drop from stride=1 to stride=16:
\begin{equation}
  \TDS(\mathcal{D}) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|} \sum_{m\in\mathcal{M}} \bigl[\mathrm{Acc}(m,\mathcal{D},s{=}1) - \mathrm{Acc}(m,\mathcal{D},s{=}16)\bigr]^+
  \label{eq:tds}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]^+$ excludes feature-collapsed models. \TDS{} is a \emph{dataset property}: it measures the dataset's intrinsic temporal frequency demand independently of any single model.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

For each model-dataset pair, two-way ANOVA decomposes variance into coverage and stride components (effect size $\eta^2$). Levene's test detects per-class variance inflation under sparse sampling. Post-hoc Welch's $t$-tests with Bonferroni correction identify aliasing ``cliffs.''

\subsection{Entropy-Based Routing}

Given temporal-sweep results at two operating points---cheap: cov=100\%/$s$=4 (4 effective frames); dense: cov=100\%/$s$=1 (16 frames)---we route video $v$ by threshold: \[ \hat{y}(v) = \begin{cases}
    \hat{y}_\text{cheap} & \text{if } \max_c\,p(c\mid v,s{=}4) > \tau \\
    \hat{y}_\text{dense} & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\] We sweep $\tau\in[0.10,\,0.95]$ and report the Pareto-optimal point at avg frames~$\leq8$. No retraining is required; only the existing temporal-sweep inference is reused.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Temporal Demand Score Validates Architecture-Independent Ranking}
\label{sec:results_tds}

Figure~\ref{fig:tds_spectral} (left) shows the \TDS{} ranking across 8 datasets. \textbf{FineGym (55.9pp)} is the most important new finding in this dimension: fine-grained gymnastics phase transitions (floor exercise, parallel bars, vault) \emph{rival} sign language (AUTSL, 53.0pp) as the highest-demand domain, confirming that \emph{sport} is not inherently low-TDS---it depends on the granularity of the classification task. AUTSL requires dense sampling to discriminate fine handshape transitions (53.0pp), but FineGym's sub-second phase boundaries are comparably demanding. UCF-101 (4.9pp) is primarily appearance-driven: object identity alone suffices.

Crucially, the ranking for positions 3--8 is \emph{exactly} stable under leave-one-architecture-out: removing any single one of the 8 architectures and recomputing \TDS{} leaves SSv2, DriveAct, Diving-48, HMDB-51, EPIC-Kitchens, and UCF-101 in the same relative order in all 8 cases ($\rho=1.00$ for 7 of the 8 leave-one-out pools). The exception is the \#1/\#2 position: removing VideoMamba specifically swaps FineGym and AUTSL ($\rho=0.976$), because VideoMamba is comparatively robust on AUTSL (16.0pp, near the bottom of VideoMamba's own range) but comparatively fragile on FineGym (40.8pp, its single worst dataset)---so its inclusion is what tips FineGym narrowly above AUTSL. We report FineGym and AUTSL as effectively co-equal highest-demand domains rather than claiming a definitive order between them; full leave-one-out results are in supplementary~S11.

\paragraph{Spectral validation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:tds_spectral} (right) shows the Pearson correlation between per-class optical-flow magnitude and aliasing loss across all 8 datasets, including FineGym. AUTSL ($r=0.285$, $p<0.001$) and DriveAct ($r=0.327$, $p=0.063$) show the strongest flow-aliasing correlation. EPIC-Kitchens shows near-zero correlation ($r=-0.002$), suggesting that in egocentric datasets, background camera motion dominates optical flow, decoupling it from action-relevant temporal demand (Table~\ref{tab:spectral}).

\subsection{Cross-Architecture Temporal Aliasing}
\label{sec:results_temporal}

\paragraph{Attention type predicts aliasing.}
Table~\ref{tab:e1_full} shows accuracy at stride=1 and the drop at stride=16 for all 8 models across 8 datasets. The central finding is that aliasing tracks \emph{attention mechanism type} more closely than architecture family (Fig.~\ref{fig:aliasing_curves}); we evaluate 8 architectures and cannot rule out that other unobserved factors (pretraining objective, parameter count, tokenization) covary with this grouping.

\textbf{Robust group} (mean drop $\leq$12pp): TimeSformer (divided attention, 10.3pp) and VideoMamba (SSM, 11.4pp, all 8 datasets). Both preserve global temporal context under aggressive subsampling---VideoMamba via recurrent state accumulation, TimeSformer via full-clip cross-frame attention.

VideoMamba's robustness is markedly \emph{bimodal} across datasets. On 5 of 8 datasets it aliases barely at all: UCF-101 (0.8pp), HMDB-51 (3.6pp), EPIC-Kitchens (1.2pp), Diving-48 (4.9pp), DriveAct (9.6pp)---averaging 4.0pp, which would make it the most robust model by a large margin. But on the three highest-TDS domains, VideoMamba suffers substantially: AUTSL (16.0pp), SSv2 (14.1pp), and \textbf{FineGym (40.8pp)}, lifting its full 8-dataset average to 11.4pp.

\textbf{FineGym most clearly inverts the SSM-vs-Transformer ranking:} TimeSformer drops 36.1pp on FineGym vs.\ VideoMamba's 40.8pp, a 4.7pp gap favoring divided attention. The same direction holds, more weakly, on SSv2 (VideoMamba 14.1pp vs.\ TimeSformer 12.8pp) and is tied on AUTSL (both 16.0pp)---so divided attention is not uniquely advantaged on FineGym, but the advantage is largest there. We hypothesize this traces to a common cause: VideoMamba's recurrent state integrates temporal evidence as a running average, which would smooth over high-frequency phase transitions rather than preserving the key-frame signal, while TimeSformer's cross-frame attention can \emph{selectively} attend to the most discriminative frames. We have not directly measured state dynamics or attention maps to confirm this mechanism; it remains a plausible explanation consistent with the accuracy patterns rather than a verified causal account.

The SSv2 result (14.1pp for VideoMamba) extends beyond frequency: SSv2 tests causal \emph{temporal ordering} (``move cup left then right''), not just temporal density. SSM recurrence is not inherently order-preserving when frames are skipped---the state integrates a corrupted temporal sequence. This explains why VideoMamba aliases on SSv2 despite low-frequency class prototypes, while appearance-dominated UCF-101 remains unaffected (0.8pp).

The VideoMamba/AUTSL value (65.6\%/16.0pp) uses the AUTSL-fine-tuned checkpoint at native resolution (224px), fine-tuned with the same 10-epoch recipe as every other model (supplementary~S8); a K400-pretrained VideoMamba checkpoint without AUTSL fine-tuning collapses to 0.4\% on AUTSL's 226 sign-language classes and is not used.

\textbf{Fragile group} (mean drop $\geq$22pp): SlowFast (42.1pp), VideoMAE (32.8pp), ViViT (30.0pp), R2+1D (30.0pp), R3D-18 (29.7pp), MC3-18 (22.6pp). ViViT's failure---despite being a Transformer---is the \emph{ViViT anomaly}: its factorized attention processes spatial features frame-independently, leaving the temporal aggregation module vulnerable to frame dropout.

\paragraph{Architecture-specific failure modes.}
\textit{SlowFast cliff at stride 2$\to$4}: The fast pathway requires 32 frames and the slow pathway requires 8. At stride=4, effective inputs drop to 8 and 2 respectively---both are simultaneously starved, producing a precipitous cliff one stride earlier than other architectures. At stride=4 on AUTSL, SlowFast drops from 77\% to 41\%---a 36pp step. On FineGym, SlowFast reaches the highest base accuracy (78.2\%) but drops 71.5pp at stride=16---the largest single-dataset aliasing loss in the entire benchmark.

\textit{VideoMAE temporal paradox}: VideoMAE achieves the highest base accuracy on UCF-101 (95.4\%) and FineGym (78.0\%) yet suffers among the largest aliasing losses. The masked autoencoder objective teaches spatial robustness by reconstructing patches under heavy masking, but does not prepare the model for \emph{temporal} gaps, which differ fundamentally from spatially masked patches.

\textit{VideoMamba bimodal profile}: VideoMamba's aliasing is strongly bimodal across datasets (avg 4.0pp over 5 domains; 40.8pp on FineGym, 16.0pp on AUTSL, 14.1pp on SSv2)---see Discussion for hypothesized failure modes.

\paragraph{Variance inflation.}
Levene's test shows that stride significantly inflates inter-class accuracy variance in 67\% of model-dataset pairs ($p<0.05$, Bonferroni-corrected). A model that \emph{on average} achieves 70\% under sparse sampling but with a variance ratio of $2\times$ will unpredictably fail on subsets of classes, a risk that mean accuracy alone does not reveal.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does attention type predict aliasing?}
\emph{Divided attention} (TimeSformer) computes temporal context globally at each block: every frame attends to all others, \emph{selectively} weighting the most discriminative instants and suppressing the impact of any single missing frame. \emph{Factorized attention} (ViViT) extracts spatial features frame-independently before temporal aggregation; when frames are dropped, the temporal module receives impoverished per-frame representations it cannot recover from. \emph{SSM recurrence} (VideoMamba) integrates temporal context as a running state: on slowly-varying domains, missing frames perturb the state boundedly.

Full characterization across all 8 datasets reveals VideoMamba has a \textbf{bimodal} aliasing profile---robust on 5 datasets (avg 4.0pp), severely aliased on 3. On \textbf{FineGym} (40.8pp), gymnastics phase transitions at $\sim$6--8\,Hz exceed the SSM's integration bandwidth: the running state absorbs inter-phase evidence into a temporally smeared representation. TimeSformer (36.1pp) outperforms VideoMamba on FineGym by the widest margin (4.7pp) and also on SSv2 (12.8pp vs.\ 14.1pp), with FineGym being the dataset that most dramatically exposes SSM bandwidth limits relative to divided attention. On \textbf{SSv2} (14.1pp), the failure mode is different: SSv2 requires discriminating fine-grained \emph{temporal ordering} (``move cup left-then-right'' vs.\ ``right-then-left''), and SSM recurrence over a subsampled sequence corrupts exactly this ordering signal, while TimeSformer's cross-frame attention can detect the direction of motion even from sparse frames. On \textbf{AUTSL} (16.0pp), sign-language handshape discrimination requires rapid fine-grained spatial-temporal transitions; the SSM state integrates these into a blurred trajectory that loses handshape identity under subsampling, unlike Transformers whose attention localizes the most discriminative hand configuration across the sequence. Together, FineGym, SSv2, and AUTSL expose three SSM failure modes: \emph{bandwidth} (too-fast transitions), \emph{order sensitivity} (causal temporal reasoning), and \emph{spatial-temporal precision} (fine-grained trajectory identity)---a taxonomy not previously characterized in the video literature~\cite{huang2018makes,sevilla2021only}.

\paragraph{Spatial robustness mirrors temporal robustness.}
Cross-resolution results confirmed across all 8 datasets show that the same architectural property governing temporal aliasing also governs spatial scale sensitivity, with one exception. CNNs' strided convolutions learn features at a specific spatial scale; off-resolution, spatial frequency patterns shift relative to learned filter banks, causing catastrophic accuracy loss. Global attention aggregates tokens irrespective of absolute spatial scale, giving Transformers uniform robustness ($\leq$8pp on 7 of 8 datasets). VideoMamba's spatial robustness is \emph{not} uniform: it matches Transformers on SSv2, UCF-101, and Diving-48 ($\leq$7.5pp), but degrades sharply on DriveAct ($-22.8$pp) and EPIC-Kitchens ($-17.3$pp)---a spatial echo of the same bimodal pattern observed temporally, suggesting the SSM's recurrent state is sensitive to scale shifts specifically on fine-grained, cluttered scenes (in-cabin activities, egocentric cooking) where small objects carry the discriminative signal. CNNs require target-resolution fine-tuning to recover performance; Transformers are already scale-invariant; VideoMamba is scale-invariant only on coarse-grained domains.

The ViViT result is particularly instructive: despite being a Transformer and temporally fragile, ViViT is \emph{spatially robust} ($-1.9$pp max drop on SSv2), confirming that patch tokenization followed by attention is the source of spatial invariance, \emph{independent} of whether temporal attention is factorized or divided. Temporal and spatial robustness arise from different architectural properties.

\paragraph{Resolution--latency--sampling convergence.}
An underappreciated interaction arises when spatial resolution, latency, and sampling density are optimized jointly. Target-resolution fine-tuning turns resolution into a \emph{continuous} cost dial: CNNs at 96px (retrained, 64.9\% avg) run at 0.89ms, competitive with Transformer cross-resolution inference at 224px (71.4\%, 2.82ms)---a 3.2$\times$ latency reduction for a 6.5pp accuracy cost. At 48px, CNN fine-tuning reaches 49.3\% at 0.61ms---well below the floor of any Transformer configuration, enabling deployment in sub-millisecond embedded budgets where Transformers are entirely infeasible. On the temporal axis, a symmetric convergence occurs: temporally \emph{robust} architectures at aggressive stride can match \emph{fragile} ones at dense stride. R2+1D at stride=1 achieves 42.6\% on SSv2; TimeSformer with entropy routing at avg 7.7 frames reaches 42.5\%---identical accuracy at less than half the frames. This \emph{model convergence} under complementary operating points means the effective accuracy frontier is broader than any single architecture achieves alone: practitioners should search jointly over architecture, resolution, and sampling density rather than treating them as independent axes.

\paragraph{Nyquist-aware deployment.}
From \TDS{} and measured latency, we derive principled deployment rules:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{High-TDS domains} (FineGym, sign language, $\TDS{}>40$pp): require
  stride$\leq$2. At 30fps source, this means $\geq$15fps effective. On a server (RTX 4090), VideoMAE at 112--224px ($\leq$4ms) or TimeSformer ($\leq$4.3ms) meet any realistic latency budget with Nyquist compliance. On edge devices (Jetson Nano), only CNNs at $\leq$96px (0.61--0.89ms $\times$50 $\approx$30--45ms) remain viable; VideoMamba (400--770ms on Nano) is infeasible.
\item \textbf{Moderate-TDS domains} (SSv2, DriveAct, $15<\TDS{}\leq$40pp): stride$\leq$4
  is safe. Entropy routing routes 70--88\% of videos to 4-frame cheap inference.
\item \textbf{Low-TDS domains} (UCF-101, EPIC-Kitchens, $\TDS{}\leq$15pp): stride$\leq$16;
  any architecture at any resolution in 48--224px works. CNN at 48px (0.61ms on server) provides an extreme efficiency option with $<$10pp loss.
\item \textbf{Resolution choice}: Deploy Transformers at any resolution 48--224px
  without retraining ($\leq$8pp loss). CNNs must be fine-tuned at the target resolution (especially critical for fine-grained domains where off-native loss $>$70pp).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The ViViT anomaly.}
ViViT's fragility is a design lesson. The factorized attention topology was motivated by computational efficiency but inadvertently creates a temporal reasoning bottleneck: CLS tokens passed to the temporal Transformer carry impoverished representations when frames are dropped. Practitioners should not assume ``Transformer'' implies ``temporally robust''; the attention factorization strategy must be explicitly considered.

\paragraph{VideoMamba's latency paradox.}
VideoMamba achieves competitive temporal robustness yet is 4--8$\times$ slower than VideoMAE on CUDA. SSMs' theoretical FLOPs advantage materializes primarily on sequence-length-sensitive hardware (e.g., long-sequence CPUs or dedicated accelerators), not on modern GPU matrix engines optimized for parallel attention. For GPU-deployed applications, VideoMAE dominates on accuracy-per-ms; TimeSformer dominates on robustness-per-ms for high-TDS domains.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\emph{Spectral validation}: flow-magnitude correlation ($r=0.14$--$0.33$) is exploratory; egomotion-compensated flow and FineGym per-class analysis are pending. \emph{Clip duration}: correlation ($r=-0.3$ to $-0.8$) reported without controls for class distribution; the SSv2 3--6s bin reversal is unexplained. \emph{Routing}: the global threshold $\tau$ is swept; per-dataset calibration could close the 4.8pp oracle gap. The two-tier budget (4f/16f) is fixed.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the first large-scale cross-architecture study of spatiotemporal aliasing in video action recognition. Evaluating 8 architectures across 8 diverse datasets (8,000+ configurations), our findings establish: (\textit{i}) aliasing is strongly associated with attention mechanism type rather than architecture family, with the ViViT anomaly demonstrating that Transformer $\neq$ robust; (\textit{ii}) the \TDS{} metric ranks 8 datasets consistently under leave-one-architecture-out for 6 of 8 datasets ($\rho{=}1.00$), with FineGym (55.9pp) and sign language (AUTSL, 53.0pp) as co-equal highest-demand domains---their exact order is sensitive to VideoMamba's inclusion---establishing fine-grained sport as a previously underappreciated high-aliasing regime; (\textit{iii}) full 8-dataset coverage of VideoMamba reveals a bimodal aliasing profile: highly robust on 5 domains (avg 4.0pp) but severely aliased on FineGym (40.8pp), AUTSL (16.0pp), and SSv2 (14.1pp)---exposing SSM limits in high-frequency phase detection, sign-language handshape discrimination, and causal temporal ordering respectively, and making TimeSformer the superior model on those critical domains; (\textit{iv}) target-resolution fine-tuning closes the CNN resolution gap by up to 39pp while Transformers require no retraining; (\textit{v}) VideoMAE is the best accuracy-per-ms Transformer (1.6--2.8ms vs.\ VideoMamba's 8--15ms) despite VideoMamba's theoretical efficiency claims; and (\textit{vi}) entropy routing reduces inference cost by 52\% with no accuracy penalty. Together these results provide a principled, data-grounded framework for architecture and resolution selection under Nyquist sampling constraints.
